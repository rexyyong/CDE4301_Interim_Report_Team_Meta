1. Introduction (Maia & Jonathan)

The NUS-Stanford Global Engineering Design Innovation Programme is a collaborative effort between the 2 universities, where project teams, together with an industry partner, work to solve a problem provided by the industry partner. The prompt assigned to us is as follows: This project explores how smart glasses, sensors, and AI can augment and enrich social interactions. Students should consider diverse user groups to uncover unmet needs, then propose solutions that address those needs through the design of the user experience and hardware/software capabilities.

Our team, under the guidance of our professor and advisor, Professor Khoo Eng Tat and Graham Zhu, started by exploring many potential user groups. We then delved deeper into a select few with the most potential to work on. Initial groups we explored included the visually-impaired, hearing-impaired and networking professionals. After weeks of research and deliberation, we have decided to focus our efforts on the topic of elderly loneliness, an increasingly prevalent problem around the world and in Singapore.

In 2023, the World Health Organisation declared loneliness a global public health concern [1]. Around the world, countries have created task forces to tackle this issue specifically [2]. Japan and the United Kingdom have established a new role called the Minister for Loneliness, which highlights the increased focus that these countries are putting on addressing it [2]. In Singapore, about an average of 50% or more people feel socially isolated and coupled with the ageing population, it puts a strain on our community to reach out to the elderly and keep them engaged [3]. The truth of the matter is: the silver tsunami is increasingly enveloping our populations and exacerbating this problem in the elderly.

Around 14.1% of the elderly above 70 live with a mental disorder, with one of the causes being social isolation, with a staggering suicide rate of 16.6% [4]. They also experience health consequences such as increased risk of developing Alzheimer’s disease.

Therefore, this project aims to create a new system using extended reality (XR) and artificial intelligence (AI) to bridge the gap faced by the elderly when building and maintaining social interactions.

2. Understanding the Elderly Loneliness Problem (Thulasidas & Jonathan)

2.1. Literature Review

Social Isolation vs Loneliness

According to Puyane et al, social isolation refers to an objective lack of social contact, while loneliness is the subjective experience of unmet social needs [5]. A study identified key factors contributing to elderly loneliness, such as: being old, a widow/divorced, having a lower level of education and/or income, living alone, having a chronic disease, poor self-perceived health, lack of visits by relatives or acquaintances, dissatisfaction with the place of living, and being fully dependent while performing daily activities [6].

What can be done to combat this? It turns out that having more frequent, more pleasant and in-person social interactions, as well as interactions with friends and family specifically, significantly predicted lower momentary loneliness a few hours later [7]. Additionally, home-based interventions were found to significantly increase older adults’ social connectedness (social support and social engagement) and reduce their loneliness and depressive symptoms; and interventions using mixed platforms were more favourable, which include face-to-face and digital delivery [8].

Active ageing, Befriending, and Care and support in the Community model (ABC)

Singapore's strategy to combat social isolation employs the 'ABC' framework, which differentiates its approach across a continuum of need: Active Ageing functions as the primary preventive strategy for all older adults, promoting participation in social, cultural, spiritual, and civic affairs to maintain overall health and quality of life, thereby stemming the initial onset of isolation [9]. In contrast, Befriending is a targeted intervention focusing on older people already identified as socially isolated, creating a "purpose-built, one-on-one relationship" to provide direct companionship and fill the social gap for those who lack a personal network [10], [11]. Finally, the Community pillar, facilitated by groups such as Resident Networks and the People's Association, offers accessible programs, such as fitness classes and arts and crafts sessions, that serve as supported group-based environments to foster new social connections and encourage active engagement among seniors in their neighbourhood [12].

Befriender Model Overview

As Singapore's traditional family structures evolve and fewer relatives live together, the societal need for companionship services has grown, prompting social service agencies to complement their core programmes with befriending [13], [14]. This service targets older adults who exhibit at least one of the four risk factors: living alone, physical or cognitive impairment, limited social networks, or serving as a primary caregiver. These factors reduce the daily social contact and emotional support that the individuals need.

In response, the Agency for Integrated Care (AIC) defines a befriender primarily as a companion who provides sustained emotional support and cultivates meaningful social bonds. Through regular home visits and conversations, befrienders work to reduce loneliness and encourage seniors to participate in activities outside the home [14].

Gap in the Current Model

Current befriending services face a twofold crisis in logistics and design. Agencies struggle to recruit and retain volunteers for long-term commitments because the model requires frequent visits, such as twice weekly over six months or longer, and few people can sustain this level of involvement [15]. Turnover matters because isolated seniors need time and a steady presence to build trust, and for many, consistency is not a preference but a source of personal security. Knowing that someone will reliably visit or call creates perceived dependability, which is important for befriending to be successful [16].

Despite these operational challenges, research on the befriending model often shows weak and inconsistent long-term effectiveness [17]. This failure stems from a disconnect between the service's design and what seniors actually value [17].

Practitioners report that the loneliest seniors do not want a random visitor; they want a genuine connection with peers who share similar backgrounds and life experiences [13]. Research shows that effective befriending does not happen by chance and depends on activating specific mechanisms that current models often ignore [17]. The mechanisms include empathy, reciprocity and autonomy. Shared experience generates empathy, for example, when a befriender and a senior both live with arthritis. Shared characteristics foster reciprocity, enabling volunteers and users to form mutual two-way relationships. Long-term flexible relationships support autonomy, while rigid, predefined priorities do not. To be effective, programmes must therefore prioritise deliberate matching and sustained relationships.

In summary, the current befriending model fails because it is built on a "buddy" system of random, high-turnover volunteers, while the loneliest seniors need consistent, peer-to-peer connections to feel secure and understood.

2.4. Synthesis of Data

Drawing on our primary and secondary research, we adopt a two-pronged approach to address elderly loneliness. First, we support older adults in leading more connected lives through technology and human networks. Given persistent manpower shortages, the current befriender model cannot reliably deliver sustained companionship; technology can complement it and reduce reliance on volunteers.

Second, we empower social workers to deliver more timely, personalised support. Heavy caseloads and administrative burden constrain service quality. Hence, by streamlining workflows and providing decision support, social workers can devote more time to high-value interactions that help seniors reconnect with the community.



3. Supporting the Elderly (Jonathan and Thulasidas)

In this section, we will explore the concepts related to the elderly (above age 65) that can address the gaps identified in the previous section.

3.1. Problem Statement for the Elderly

Socially isolated elderly in Singapore need reliable, long-term companionship to reduce loneliness and preserve their overall well-being. Existing befriending models fail to deliver sustained, well-matched relationships due to volunteer shortages and the short-term matching of random volunteers who do not meet the needs of the elderly.

3.2. Elderly Needs

To address the problem statement, the solution must address the following elderly needs:

  1. Elderly users need a non-judgmental space where they can express views and emotions without criticism or stigma.
  2. Elderly users need companionship access that is consistently available when they reach out.
  3. Elderly users need an interaction channel that is accessible and intuitive for low digital-literacy users and those with sensory or motor limitations.

3.3. Case Studies on AI Companions

A two-week ChatGPT intervention with 20 older adults reduced the proportion scoring above the UCLA loneliness threshold of 43, a high degree of loneliness, from 100% to 32%. Participants attributed this change to the system’s constant availability and perceived dependability, noting that “ChatGPT is always there to chat with me, no matter the time or day.”

They valued the AI’s ability to hold context-rich conversations across social, recreational and informational topics, saying “I liked that ChatGPT could talk about a lot of different things, from the weather to my favourite TV shows,” and they described most interactions as social, recreational, educational or emotional in nature [18].

Users also highlighted practical benefits, including instant information retrieval, calendar management and medication reminders, and they said the AI’s patient, non-judgmental responses encouraged disclosure and emotional sharing. Several participants called the system a confidant, reporting that “it understands me in ways that no one else can,” and many summed up the experience with, “It’s comforting to know that there is someone who listens and understands, even if it’s an AI.” Together, these findings show that consistent availability, empathetic tone, contextual knowledge and being an easy information source combine to produce meaningful companionship and measurable reductions in loneliness [18].

A six-month pilot of an Alexa AI assistant with 105 homebound older adults produced a mean reduction of 10.87 points on the UCLA Loneliness Scale, offering a real-world benchmark for medium-term impact [19]. The device functioned as a companion, facilitating communication with family and friends and providing practical support such as medication and appointment reminders, suggesting that conversational presence combined with task assistance can improve both social connectedness and day-to-day functioning [19].

With the research being positive on the use of chatbots to alleviate the perception of loneliness the next promising direction include prioritising low-friction delivery, such as TV and natural speech or gesture interfaces to maximise uptake, leveraging AI’s always-on availability to provide consistent perceived dependability that builds trust, and using proactive but tunable nudges to increase engagement while monitoring intrusion [20],[21].

This gap creates a clear design space for alternative solutions that reliably deliver consistent, judgment-free social contact that the elderly can relate to, which could potentially nudge them to carry out activities that reduce social isolation.

3.4. Development of Concepts

From the previous section, conversational AI can help alleviate loneliness among older adults. When paired with the hands-free interface of AR glasses, this approach can significantly reduce the friction in user interaction. Building on this idea, we propose two concepts designed to address the companionship gap faced by socially isolated elderly individuals.

  1. Befriender at Your Service: This concept envisions AR glasses equipped with a familiar avatar and a conversational AI tailored to the needs of older adults. The system sustains companionship by proactively initiating check-ins, recalling past conversations and personal preferences. The AI will employ non-judgmental, validation-oriented language to ensure users feel heard and respected. Its always-on availability and predictable interaction cadence offer a stable alternative to volunteer befrienders, helping mitigate the inconsistencies of current models. The interface prioritises accessibility through hands-free, low-friction controls, making it suitable for users with limited technological familiarity or physical impairments.
  1. Connecting You: This concept features an AR heads-up display that highlights a curated list of close contacts, alongside real-time availability indicators. Users can initiate a voice or video call with a single spoken command, reducing the uncertainty and hesitation associated with interrupting others. By removing technical barriers, such as navigating apps or contact lists, the system streamlines the connection process. Additionally, the interface will show nearby social activities aligned with the user’s interests, along with one-command RSVP and navigation functions. This reduces cognitive load and bridges the gap between event discovery and participation, promoting more frequent and meaningful social engagement.

3.5. Critical Function Prototyping

To evaluate the effectiveness of our concepts, we conducted critical function prototyping for both ideas. For Befriender at Your Service, we tested whether a conversational AI could sustain natural and engaging dialogue with elderly users. For Connecting You, we assessed whether real-time visibility of loved ones’ availability would encourage more frequent user-initiated contact.

3.5.1. Befriender at Your Service

We tested three conversational AI models—ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude—with elderly participants using an iPad interface. Each session lasted 1 hour, with each model being tested for 20 minutes, during which we recorded the following metrics:

  1. Number of conversation interruptions/drop-offs
  2. Number of misunderstandings by AI/user during the conversation
  3. Ratio of AI-to-user speaking time
  4. Number of times the user laughed/smiled (used as a proxy for engagement and enjoyment)

To ensure consistency of the experiment, we prompted the AI based on what makes a good befriender: “You are a compassionate befriender speaking with an older adult in Singapore. Listen, validate, and keep the conversation going. Speak at a moderate pace. Use simple, open-ended questions and let the person finish before you respond. Be empathetic and match their pace and vocabulary. Ask gentle follow-up questions that invite storytelling, and encourage softly if they hesitate. Avoid judging, lecturing, or arguing. If they mention harm, severe distress, or a medical emergency, escalate to a human contact. Keep replies brief and wait for their response.”\[22].

3.5.2. Connecting You

To evaluate the effectiveness of displaying loved ones’ availability, we conducted a two-day experiment with an elderly participant. On Day 1, we recorded the baseline number of calls made. On Day 2, we introduced the Connecting You prototype by displaying a real-time availability indicator on an iPad positioned beside the living room television, where the elderly will frequently walk past.

The status of loved ones was updated manually via a shared Google Sheet, enabling family members to reflect their availability in real-time. Throughout the day, we tracked both the number of calls made and the number of passive interactions with the iPad (e.g., glances, touches) to gauge the user's level of engagement with the display.

This setup aimed to test whether easy access to live availability information could lower social hesitation and increase the likelihood of initiating contact.

3.6. Results

3.6.1. Befriender at Your Service

User testing for Befriender at Your Service yielded promising insights into the elderly’s receptiveness to AI-mediated conversations. All three participants described the AI as “easy to talk to” and appreciated its consistently polite and patient tone. They valued having someone to converse with on their own terms, without fear of judgment. Even though they knew the AI was not human, it offered a sense of companionship, making them feel “entertained and heard.”.

The AI has also demonstrated practical utility. In one example, a participant asked where to learn pickleball. The AI responded with a list of nearby facilities, and the participant remarked that this was helpful because he "would not have known how to search those on the internet." This highlights the AI’s potential to bridge digital literacy gaps and provide accessible information in real time.

Quantitative findings (see Table 1) revealed key areas for improvement:

  • Interruption vs. Enjoyment: A negative correlation was observed between the number of conversation interruptions and user enjoyment (measured by laughter/smiles). Interruptions often occurred when the elderly spoke before the AI finished, leading to awkward pauses and inevitably causing the conversation to break down. Claude AI had the fewest interruptions due to its push-to-send interface, which reduced accidental input from background noise—a common issue with ChatGPT and Gemini, which rely on continuous voice detection.
  • Misunderstanding vs. Enjoyment: Fewer misunderstandings also corresponded with higher engagement. AIs often struggled to interpret local expressions or Singlish, which limited their ability to maintain shared context. Participants noted that these moments made them feel “less understood” or “distant” from the AI.
  • Turn-taking Dynamics: Participants reported that the AI sometimes “talked too much,” creating an unnatural conversation flow. Long monologues from the AI made them feel like they were waiting for their turn, which contradicted typical human conversational patterns where interruptions or overlaps are common.

In contrast, the Connecting You prototype yielded limited impact. As shown in Table 2, the number of calls remained the same. However, the participant interacted with the iPad nine times, suggesting passive interest in the display.

The elderly participant shared that while the status display helped her stay updated on her loved ones’ busyness, she still hesitated to call due to a lack of conversation topics, saying she “didn’t know what to talk about besides asking how they are.” The display thus offered emotional reassurance but failed to overcome deeper social hesitation or conversation anxiety.

Additionally, loved ones found it tedious to update their availability manually via Google Sheets, often forgetting to change their status after completing tasks. This friction further limited the real-time reliability of the system.

However, we acknowledge that the small sample size and short duration limit the generalisability of our findings for Connecting You.

3.7. Refinement of Concept

Findings from the critical function prototyping suggest that Befriender at Your Service holds strong potential in alleviating perceived loneliness among the elderly. Participants appreciated having an always-available companion who responded politely and engaged in conversations on their terms. This constant, non-judgmental presence offered a sense of comfort, agency, and emotional connection.

Conversely, the Connecting You concept did not increase the frequency of user-initiated calls. While the live availability display provided some peace of mind by allowing the elderly to stay updated on their loved ones’ busyness, it did not serve as an effective nudge to initiate contact. However, one element of the Connecting You concept, AI-curated activity suggestions, was well-received from our test in the Befriender at Your Service. Participants appreciated learning about nearby events aligned with their interests, given their limited digital literacy, which often prevents them from discovering such opportunities on their own.

Based on these insights, we propose merging the two concepts. We will integrate the proactive, personalised event-sharing feature from Connecting You into Befriender at Your Service. The resulting AI companion will not only offer conversational support but also serve as a contextual recommender, nudging users towards meaningful activities in their community.

To further refine this concept, we will investigate several key areas in the following semester:

  • Medium preference: Identify the most suitable hardware interface (e.g., AR glasses, tablet, TV overlay) for elderly users.
  • Localisation: Develop a culturally relevant AI that understands and responds naturally to Singlish, local references, and Singaporean conversational norms.
  • Content design: Discover common conversation topics that resonate with elderly users and determine preferred formats for sharing programme information.
  • Nudging: Assess the effectiveness of using AI to proactively surface interest-aligned programmes and determine whether such nudges increase the likelihood of participation in social or community activities.

4. Tackling Social Isolation through Empowering Social Workers

4.1. Pretext and Problem Statement for Social Workers (Maia)

We will now be exploring the social workers' portion of our two-pronged approach.

4.1.1. Role of Social Workers

According to the National Council of Social Services (NCSS), the role of a social worker is complex as they juggle many responsibilities, such as working with clients to set goals and develop case plans, facilitating access to resources and maintaining and submitting social reports, just to name a few [23].

We have broken it down to 3 key client-facing tasks they carry out to provide holistic care to the elderly:

  1. Case work and counselling
  2. Advocacy and resource linkage
  3. Community development

For case work and counselling, they are required to provide end-to-end case management and support and an appropriate level of care through assessment, care planning and interventions. This step is further broken down [24], with key points such as:

  • Establish rapport and build professional relationships
  • Identify issues related to the problem and gather relevant information from clients, and conduct needs and intake assessment
  • Provide recommendations to modify case plans
  • Document and maintain case files and prepare reports

For advocacy and resource linkage, they act as middlemen to facilitate the flow of resources from other agencies and tap on community resources to provide intervention support for the clients. Social workers act on behalf of their clients in order to access needed resources, services or to influence policy change, which includes filing case reports. They have to liaise with agencies and make service referrals [25].

For community development, social workers are required to design, develop and implement community-specific development programmes after assessing the needs, while also participating in community development initiatives. They have to track outcomes from these initiatives and collect data as well.

4.1.2. Current Gaps faced by Social Workers

The scope of their job is incredibly wide, notwithstanding the fact that they usually handle dozens of cases simultaneously. An article published by CNA cites multiple social workers who are incredibly burnt out due to the workload. [26] For example, a crisis by one of the clients may require bringing 3 children to the hospital at 4 am, only for their workday to begin in 5 hours. It would be understandable that an industry veteran would be exhausted when pushed to their limits after several years. The article also talks about the excessive caseload a social worker faces, handling 30 to 50 cases at a given time. They also said: “These ‘excessive’ caseloads ‘directly hurt their ability to do a good job’, as they have less attention to give to each case.”

Each client also comes with their various admin tasks, as mentioned in the section on case work above. Social workers are also required to maintain these case files, often having to transcribe meetings with clients, which are incredibly time-consuming and tedious, on top of excessive paperwork. [26] Coupled with the massive number of clients they are required to handle, social workers are often overwhelmed by admin tasks.

Thus, the overwhelming workload due to high case load and tedious admin tasks is often cited as the key reason that impedes the ability of social workers to perform their best.

4.1.3. Problem Statement for Social Worker

Every week, social workers supporting the 223 Active Ageing Centres (AACs) in Singapore [27] assist numerous different elderly in the neighbourhood with different circumstances and personalities. With so many different personal circumstances, it is often difficult for the staff to provide personalised social assistance to the elderly. As a result, the need for creating personalised approaches significantly increases the complexity of the staff’s job scope, leaving them with less time to support the elderly.

4.3. Concept Exploration for Underserved Outcomes of Social Workers (Rex)

Based on the top 4 underserved outcomes, we identified opportunities to help social workers do their jobs better and faster. 4 key concepts were then created.

4.3.1. Concepts for Aiding Social Workers

  1. Fact assistant: A distraction-free prompter that is able to advise the social worker on topics based on the known historical facts of the elderly.
  2. Personal assistant: An assistant that is able to summarise the important information needed for reports post-interaction.
  3. Real-time coach: A coach that is able to advise the social worker on possible ways to handle elderly people based on known historical behaviours or logged cases.
  4. Real-time Duolingo: A language coach that is able to advise social workers on basic language terminologies and meanings based on their conversations.

4.4. Critical Experience Prototype (CEP) (Rex)

Since Fact Assistant had the highest score, our team created a critical experience prototype to test the Fact Assistant. Although the concept is for social workers, we used a befriender to gain quick insights first.

Parties involved:

  1. The AI: The “AI” provides prompts through text messages, while referring to a fact sheet. He listened to the conversation through earbuds so that he could send factual prompts according to the conversation.
  2. The “social worker”: A befriender who role-played as a social worker. He referred to an iPad for prompts while interacting.
  3. The elderly.

4.4.4. Conclusion of Results

In conclusion, the results are promising and they show us that distraction-free factual prompts can aid the “social worker” in generating topics for the conversation, and could even potentially lead to enjoyable topics. This directly addresses the respective underserved outcomes.

  1. Increase the number of topics that can be talked about with the elderly so that visits are more conversational.
  2. Increase the confidence of the social worker when dealing with the elderly

4.4.5. Shortcomings of Results

However, there are some important limitations to note.

  1. Firstly, the sample size tested is too small. We only conducted it on 1 “social worker” and 1 elderly. The small sample size cannot represent the experience of the larger population of elderly and social workers.
  2. Secondly, the tester of our CEP is not the same as the user of our product. The users of our product are meant to be social workers in the elderly field.
  3. Lastly, although the elderly and the “social worker” are strangers, they know the observer mutually. As a result, the elderly would be less inclined to feel tense because the mutual relationship already helps to establish some form of rapport.

4.5. Future Refinement of Concepts for Social Workers (Rex)

To address the downside where the user found it hard to draw connections between the current conversation and the fact, we propose combining the Fact Assistant and Real Time Coach concepts to address this. Since the Fact Assistant only provides the user with the what, the Real Time Coach can complement it by providing the how, which helps the user to connect the dots.

4.6. Next Steps for Social Workers (Rex)

Our next steps would be to further validate the findings of the Fact Assistant concept by doing the following:

  1. Firstly, to validate our results, we would need to increase our sample size.
  2. Secondly, we should now test the Fact Assistant concept on actual social workers. This would give us a more accurate representation.
  3. Lastly, to reduce the biasness of our tests, it would be important to test them on strangers.

5. Conclusion (All)

In conclusion, our two-pronged approach aims to alleviate the elderly loneliness problem through the elderly and the social workers.

Our findings indicate that elderly users are generally receptive to AI companions, particularly when interactions are consistent, empathetic, and easy to access. The AI companion shows strong potential to address the limitations of current befriending models by offering scalable, on-demand emotional support. Thereby alleviating pressure on human volunteers and social infrastructure. A key milestone in the next phase of development will be the creation of a culturally attuned "Singaporean AI" to enhance relatability and user trust.

For the social workers, we discovered that real time prompts have the potential to boost social worker interactions with the elderly. It is especially useful when the prompts are relevant to the hobbies or interests of the elderly.

Moving forward, more testing and a combination of concepts for both approaches are required to further validate our interim findings.
