2. Understanding the Elderly Loneliness Problem

2.1. Literature Review

Social Isolation vs Loneliness

According to Puyane et al, social isolation refers to an objective lack of social contact, while loneliness is the subjective experience of unmet social needs [m5]. A study [m1] identified key factors contributing to elderly loneliness, such as: being old, a widow/divorced, having a lower level of education and/or income, living alone, having a chronic disease, poor self-perceived health, lack of visits by relatives or acquaintances, dissatisfaction with the place of living, and being fully dependent while performing daily activities.

What can be done to combat this? It turns out that having more frequent, more pleasant and in-person social interactions, as well as interactions with friends and family specifically, significantly predicted lower momentary loneliness a few hours later. [m2] Additionally, home-based interventions were found to significantly increase older adults’ social connectedness (social support and social engagement) and reduce their loneliness and depressive symptoms; and interventions using mixed platforms were more favourable, which include face-to-face and digital delivery. [m3]

Active ageing, Befriending, and Care and support in the Community model (ABC)

Singapore's strategy to combat social isolation employs the 'ABC' framework, which differentiates its approach across a continuum of need: Active Ageing functions as the primary preventive strategy for all older adults, promoting participation in social, cultural, spiritual, and civic affairs to maintain overall health and quality of life, thereby stemming the initial onset of isolation [T1]. In contrast, Befriending is a targeted intervention focusing on older people already identified as socially isolated, creating a "purpose-built, one-on-one relationship" to provide direct companionship and fill the social gap for those who lack a personal network [T2], [T3]. Finally, the Community pillar, facilitated by groups such as Resident Networks and the People's Association, offers accessible programs, such as fitness classes and arts and crafts sessions, that serve as supported group-based environments to foster new social connections and encourage active engagement among seniors in their neighbourhood [T4].

Befriender Model Overview

As Singapore's traditional family structures evolve and fewer relatives live together, the societal need for companionship services has grown, prompting social service agencies to complement their core programmes with befriending [T5], [T7]. This service targets older adults who exhibit at least one of the four risk factors: living alone, physical or cognitive impairment, limited social networks, or serving as a primary caregiver. These factors reduce the daily social contact and emotional support that the individuals need.

In response, the Agency for Integrated Care (AIC) defines a befriender primarily as a companion who provides sustained emotional support and cultivates meaningful social bonds. Through regular home visits and conversations, befrienders work to reduce loneliness and encourage seniors to participate in activities outside the home [T7].

Gap in the Current Model

Current befriending services face a twofold crisis in logistics and design. Agencies struggle to recruit and retain volunteers for long-term commitments because the model requires frequent visits, such as twice weekly over six months or longer, and few people can sustain this level of involvement [T8]. Turnover matters because isolated seniors need time and a steady presence to build trust, and for many, consistency is not a preference but a source of personal security. Knowing that someone will reliably visit or call creates perceived dependability, which is important for befriending to be successful [T9].

Despite these operational challenges, research on the befriending model often shows weak and inconsistent long-term effectiveness [T6]. This failure stems from a disconnect between the service's design and what seniors actually value [T6].

Practitioners report that the loneliest seniors do not want a random visitor; they want a genuine connection with peers who share similar backgrounds and life experiences [T5]. Research shows that effective befriending does not happen by chance and depends on activating specific mechanisms that current models often ignore [T6]. The mechanisms include empathy, reciprocity and autonomy. Shared experience generates empathy, for example, when a befriender and a senior both live with arthritis. Shared characteristics foster reciprocity, enabling volunteers and users to form mutual two-way relationships. Long-term flexible relationships support autonomy, while rigid, predefined priorities do not. To be effective, programmes must therefore prioritise deliberate matching and sustained relationships.

In summary, the current befriending model fails because it is built on a "buddy" system of random, high-turnover volunteers, while the loneliest seniors need consistent, peer-to-peer connections to feel secure and understood.

2.2. Survey Data

We started our journey by interviewing diverse stakeholders, including academics, social workers, startup founders and community organisers from the People’s Association and Active Ageing Centres to gain a deeper understanding of social isolation. We also gathered direct user insights from the elderly through a card sorting activity.

With the insights tabulated in Miro , we identified categories of needs that could be broadly classified under social interactions, successful interactions, autonomy, training and support for workers, cognitive stimulation, and cultural stigma. From these categories, we developed broad-level need statements.

2.3. Needs statements generated

Social interactions Need for Relatable, Judgment-Free Conversation: The elderly prioritise relatable conversations and the freedom to talk about their own interests without being "judged or criticised." The enjoyment and relatability of the conversation, even if superficial, are often more important than its depth.

Need for Consistent, Trust-Based Relationships: The elderly struggle to build bonds with "transient" volunteers. They need consistent social support, which requires significant time ("relationship banking," "200 hours") to build trust. This is why "friends may be stronger social support than family," as family may be too tired or busy.

Need for Validation & Agency: They require "attention/reciprocation" and a sense of control. A "loss of sense of control" or threats to their "ego" can cause disengagement.

Need for Diverse & Context-Rich Activities: The elderly feel “left out" due to a "lack of diversity" in social activities, which become monotonous. They need activities curated around shared context (e.g., "Places, Pets, Plants") to foster easier, more natural interactions.
Successful interactions Need for purpose and agency: The elderly need a meaningful role in activities where their contributions are necessary, so they can feel useful and capable, and have a sense of ownership.

Need for grief and transitional support: The elderly require help to process their grief and adapt to major life changes like loss, so they can find a way to move forward and rediscover a sense of peace or new meaning.
Autonomy Need for secure, low-pressure connection: The elderly require a low-pressure way to feel connected and confirm their safety, so they and their loved ones can have peace of mind without it becoming an intrusive or dependent relationship.
Training and support for social workers Need for streamlined administrative and cognitive support: Social workers are caught in a conflict between administrative duties like following documentation formats, remembering details for "80 over patients", while also being present with the client. This is worsened by organisational pressure not to write things down during a visit, placing a heavy cognitive load on them to document everything later. This pressure, combined with KPIs, can make interactions feel "robotic" and create friction with the elderly.

Need for realistic, experiential training: There is a significant gap between theoretical university education and the practical, high-stress reality of social work. Students and new social workers "know what needs to be done but tend to overthink," often freezing because they lack the hands-on experience to "handle a variety of situations" or manage their "fears." Current AI chatbot training tools are inadequate because they lack real-time verbal interaction and personalisation, failing to mimic the "normal flow" of an unpredictable human conversation.

Need for in-the-moment decision support: Beyond just training, social workers need real-time help during stressful encounters. They need "prompts" that can help them regulate their own emotions and the elderly's emotions, allowing them to remain "grounded" and "confident" when "unexpected things occur," rather than feeling "shocked."

Need for scalable manpower solutions: There is a persistent "manpower shortage" of both professional social workers (due to staffing ratios) and volunteer befrienders. This scarcity will only get worse as the number of isolated seniors increases, amplifying the pressure on the existing workforce and making it impossible to rely on traditional, time-intensive engagement models alone.
Cognitive stimulation Need for personalised and intentional cognitive engagement: Activities must be specifically designed to be stimulating ("Intentionality is Key") and tailored to the individual's unique cognitive abilities and interests, to learn something new every day.

Need for technology-assisted autonomy: There is a clear opportunity for technology to act as a "provocateur" that helps seniors independently manage their Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (IADLs) and make supported decisions.
Cultural Stigma Need to bridge intergenerational and cultural communication gaps: Younger generations and social workers struggle to connect due to differing communication styles (e.g., "western culture" vs. local norms) and generational differences, which can lead to misunderstandings and a reluctance for the elderly to open up.

Need to overcome the stigma associated with seeking formal support: Societal norms create a significant barrier, making many seniors reluctant to seek mental health support or engage with social services, as they associate it with a loss of autonomy or social stigma.

Need for non-traditional engagement models that build shared context: Standard programs fail to attract "untapped seniors" and "male elderly." There is a need for alternative approaches, like fostering a "Kampong Spirit" or using a "shared AI," that create a natural, shared context (like "current affairs" or a shared "pet") to encourage interaction.

2.4. Synthesis of Data

Drawing on our primary and secondary research, we adopt a two-pronged approach to address elderly loneliness. First, we support older adults in leading more connected lives through technology and human networks. Given persistent manpower shortages, the current befriender model cannot reliably deliver sustained companionship; technology can complement it and reduce reliance on volunteers.

Second, we empower social workers to deliver more timely, personalised support. Heavy caseloads and administrative burden constrain service quality. Hence, by streamlining workflows and providing decision support, social workers can devote more time to high-value interactions that help seniors reconnect with the community and live with dignity.



3. Supporting the Elderly (Jonathan)

In this section, we will explore the concepts related to the elderly (above age 65) that can address the gaps identified in the previous section.

3.1. Problem Statement for the Elderly

Socially isolated elderly in Singapore need reliable, long-term companionship to reduce loneliness and preserve their overall well-being. Existing befriending models fail to deliver sustained, well-matched relationships due to volunteer shortages and the short-term matching of random volunteers who do not meet the needs of the elderly.

3.2. User Needs

To address the problem statement, the solution must address the following elderly needs:

  1. Elderly users need a non-judgmental space where they can express views and emotions without criticism or stigma.
  2. Elderly users need companionship access that is consistently available when they reach out.
  3. Elderly users need an interaction channel that is accessible and intuitive for low digital-literacy users and those with sensory or motor limitations.

3.3. Case studies on AI Companions

A two-week ChatGPT intervention with 20 older adults reduced the proportion scoring above the UCLA loneliness threshold of 43, a high degree of loneliness, from 100% to 32%. Participants attributed this change to the system’s constant availability and perceived dependability, noting that “ChatGPT is always there to chat with me, no matter the time or day.”

They valued the AI’s ability to hold context-rich conversations across social, recreational and informational topics, saying “I liked that ChatGPT could talk about a lot of different things, from the weather to my favourite TV shows,” and they described most interactions as social, recreational, educational or emotional in nature [10].

Users also highlighted practical benefits, including instant information retrieval, calendar management and medication reminders, and they said the AI’s patient, non-judgmental responses encouraged disclosure and emotional sharing. Several participants called the system a confidant, reporting that “it understands me in ways that no one else can,” and many summed up the experience with, “It’s comforting to know that there is someone who listens and understands, even if it’s an AI.” Together, these findings show that consistent availability, empathetic tone, contextual knowledge and being an easy information source combine to produce meaningful companionship and measurable reductions in loneliness [10].

A six-month pilot of an Alexa AI assistant with 105 homebound older adults produced a mean reduction of 10.87 points on the UCLA Loneliness Scale, offering a real-world benchmark for medium-term impact [11]. The device functioned as a companion, facilitating communication with family and friends and providing practical support such as medication and appointment reminders, suggesting that conversational presence combined with task assistance can improve both social connectedness and day-to-day functioning [11].

With the research being positive on the use of chatbots to alleviate the perception of loneliness the next promising direction include prioritising low-friction delivery, such as TV and natural speech or gesture interfaces to maximise uptake, leveraging AI’s always-on availability to provide consistent perceived dependability that builds trust, and using proactive but tunable nudges to increase engagement while monitoring intrusion [T13],[T15].

This gap creates a clear design space for alternative solutions that reliably deliver consistent, judgment-free social contact that the elderly can relate to, which could potentially nudge them to carry out activities that reduce social isolation.

3.4. Development of Concepts

From the previous section, conversational AI can help alleviate loneliness among older adults. When paired with the hands-free interface of AR glasses, this approach can significantly reduce the friction in user interaction. Building on this idea, we propose two concepts designed to address the companionship gap faced by socially isolated elderly individuals.

  1. Befriender at Your Service: This concept envisions AR glasses equipped with a familiar avatar and a conversational AI tailored to the needs of older adults. The system sustains companionship by proactively initiating check-ins, recalling past conversations and personal preferences. The AI will employ non-judgmental, validation-oriented language to ensure users feel heard and respected. Its always-on availability and predictable interaction cadence offer a stable alternative to volunteer befrienders, helping mitigate the inconsistencies of current models. The interface prioritises accessibility through hands-free, low-friction controls, making it suitable for users with limited technological familiarity or physical impairments.
  1. Connecting You: This concept features an AR heads-up display that highlights a curated list of close contacts, alongside real-time availability indicators. Users can initiate a voice or video call with a single spoken command, reducing the uncertainty and hesitation associated with interrupting others. By removing technical barriers, such as navigating apps or contact lists, the system streamlines the connection process. Additionally, the interface will show nearby social activities aligned with the user’s interests, along with one-command RSVP and navigation functions. This reduces cognitive load and bridges the gap between event discovery and participation, promoting more frequent and meaningful social engagement.

3.5. Critical Function Prototyping

To evaluate the effectiveness of our concepts, we conducted critical function prototyping for both ideas. For Befriender at Your Service, we tested whether a conversational AI could sustain natural and engaging dialogue with elderly users. For Connecting You, we assessed whether real-time visibility of loved ones’ availability would encourage more frequent user-initiated contact.

3.5.1. Befriender at Your Service

We tested three conversational AI models—ChatGPT, Gemini, and Claude—with elderly participants using an iPad interface. Each session lasted 1 hour, with each model being tested for 20 minutes, during which we recorded the following metrics:

  1. Number of conversation interruptions/drop-offs
  2. Number of misunderstandings by AI/user during the conversation
  3. Ratio of AI-to-user speaking time
  4. Number of times the user laughed/smiled (used as a proxy for engagement and enjoyment)

To ensure consistency of the experiment, we prompted the AI based on what makes a good befriender [T16]: “You are a compassionate befriender speaking with an older adult in Singapore. Listen, validate, and keep the conversation going. Speak at a moderate pace. Use simple, open-ended questions and let the person finish before you respond. Be empathetic and match their pace and vocabulary. Ask gentle follow-up questions that invite storytelling, and encourage softly if they hesitate. Avoid judging, lecturing, or arguing. If they mention harm, severe distress, or a medical emergency, escalate to a human contact. Keep replies brief and wait for their response.”.

3.5.2. Connecting You

To evaluate the effectiveness of displaying loved ones’ availability, we conducted a two-day experiment with an elderly participant. On Day 1, we recorded the baseline number of calls made. On Day 2, we introduced the Connecting You prototype by displaying a real-time availability indicator on an iPad positioned beside the living room television, where the elderly will frequently walk past.

The status of loved ones was updated manually via a shared Google Sheet, enabling family members to reflect their availability in real-time. Throughout the day, we tracked both the number of calls made and the number of passive interactions with the iPad (e.g., glances, touches) to gauge the user's level of engagement with the display.

This setup aimed to test whether easy access to live availability information could lower social hesitation and increase the likelihood of initiating contact.

3.6. Results

3.6.1. Befriender at Your Service

User testing for Befriender at Your Service yielded promising insights into the elderly’s receptiveness to AI-mediated conversations. All three participants described the AI as “easy to talk to” and appreciated its consistently polite and patient tone. They valued having someone to converse with on their own terms, without fear of judgment. Even though they knew the AI was not human, it offered a sense of companionship, making them feel “entertained and heard.”.

The AI has also demonstrated practical utility. In one example, a participant asked where to learn pickleball. The AI responded with a list of nearby facilities, and the participant remarked that this was helpful because he "would not have known how to search those on the internet." This highlights the AI’s potential to bridge digital literacy gaps and provide accessible information in real time.

In contrast, the Connecting You prototype yielded limited impact. As shown in Table 2, the number of calls remained the same. However, the participant interacted with the iPad nine times, suggesting passive interest in the display.

The elderly participant shared that while the status display helped her stay updated on her loved ones’ busyness, she still hesitated to call due to a lack of conversation topics, saying she “didn’t know what to talk about besides asking how they are.” The display thus offered emotional reassurance but failed to overcome deeper social hesitation or conversation anxiety.

3.7. Refinement of Concept

Findings from the critical function prototyping suggest that Befriender at Your Service holds strong potential in alleviating perceived loneliness among the elderly. Participants appreciated having an always-available companion who responded politely and engaged in conversations on their terms. This constant, non-judgmental presence offered a sense of comfort, agency, and emotional connection.

Conversely, the Connecting You concept did not increase the frequency of user-initiated calls. While the live availability display provided some peace of mind by allowing the elderly to stay updated on their loved ones’ busyness, it did not serve as an effective nudge to initiate contact. However, one element of the Connecting You concept, AI-curated activity suggestions, was well-received from our test in the Befriender at Your Service. Participants appreciated learning about nearby events aligned with their interests, given their limited digital literacy, which often prevents them from discovering such opportunities on their own.

Based on these insights, we propose merging the two concepts. We will integrate the proactive, personalised event-sharing feature from Connecting You into Befriender at Your Service. The resulting AI companion will not only offer conversational support but also serve as a contextual recommender, nudging users towards meaningful activities in their community.

To further refine this concept, we will investigate several key areas in the following semester:

  • Medium preference: Identify the most suitable hardware interface (e.g., AR glasses, tablet, TV overlay) for elderly users.
  • Localisation: Develop a culturally relevant AI that understands and responds naturally to Singlish, local references, and Singaporean conversational norms.
  • Content design: Discover common conversation topics that resonate with elderly users and determine preferred formats for sharing programme information.

4. Tackling Social Isolation through Empowering Social Workers

4.1. Pretext and Problem Statement for Social Workers

We will now be exploring the Social Workers portion of our two pronged approach.

4.1.1. Role of Social Workers

According to the National Council of Social Services (NCSS), the role of a social worker is complex as they juggle many responsibilities, such as working with clients to set goals and develop case plans, facilitating access to resources and maintaining and submitting social reports, just to name a few. [m7]

We have broken it down to 3 key client-facing tasks they carry out to provide holistic care to the elderly:

  1. Case work and counselling
    1. Engagement and assessment
    2. Case review and documentation
    3. Goal setting and intervention plans
  2. Advocacy and resource linkage
  3. Community development

For case work and counselling, they are required to provide end-to-end case management and support and an appropriate level of care through assessment, care planning and interventions. This step is further broken down [m10], with key points such as:

  • Establish rapport and build professional relationships
  • Identify issues related to the problem and gather relevant information from clients, and conduct needs and intake assessment
  • Provide recommendations to modify case plans
  • Document and maintain case files and prepare reports

For advocacy and resource linkage, they act as middlemen to facilitate the flow of resources from other agencies and tap on community resources to provide intervention support for the clients. Social workers act on behalf of their clients in order to access needed resources, services or to influence policy change, which includes filing case reports, to seek consideration for the circumstances of a client or to provide affirmative action for the client, while including them in the process so they feel empowered and assertive. They have to liaise with agencies and make service referrals. [m11]

For community development, social workers are required to design, develop and implement community-specific development programmes after assessing the needs, while also participating in community development initiatives. They have to track outcomes from these initiatives and collect data as well.

4.1.2. Current gaps faced by social workers

It is apparent that tThe scope of their job is incredibly wide, notwithstanding the fact that they usually handle dozens of cases simultaneously. An article published by CNA cites multiple social workers who are incredibly burnt out due to the workload. [m8] For example, a crisis by one of the clients may require bringing 3 children to the hospital at 4 am, only for their workday to begin in 5 hours. It would be understandable that an industry veteran would be exhausted when pushed to their limits after several years. The article also talks about the excessive caseload a social worker faces, handling 30 to 50 cases at a given time. They also said: “These ‘excessive’ caseloads ‘directly hurt their ability to do a good job’, as they have less attention to give to each case.”

Each client also comes with their various admin tasks, as mentioned in the section on case work above. Social workers are also required to maintain these case files, often having to transcribe meetings with clients, which are incredibly time-consuming and tedious, on top of excessive paperwork. [m8] Coupled with the massive number of clients they are required to handle, social workers are often overwhelmed by admin tasks.

Thus, the overwhelming workload due to high case load and tedious admin tasks are often cited as the key reasons that impede the ability of social workers to perform their best.

4.1.3. Problem Statement for Social Worker (Rex)

Every week, social workers supporting the 223 Active Ageing Centres (AACs) in Singapore [R1] assist numerous different elderly in the neighbourhood with different circumstances and personalities. With so many different personal circumstances, it is often difficult for the staff to provide personalised social assistance to the elderly. As a result, the need for creating personalised approaches significantly increases the complexity of the staff’s job scope, leaving them with less time to support the elderly.

4.3. Concept Exploration for Underserved Outcomes of Social Workers (Rex)

Based on the top 4 underserved outcomes, we identified opportunities to help Social Workers do their jobs better and faster. 4 key concepts were then created.

4.3.1. Concepts for aiding social workers

  1. Fact assistant: A distraction-free prompter that is able to advise the social worker on topics based on the known historical facts of the elderly.
  2. Personal assistant: An assistant that is able to summarise the important information needed for reports post-interaction.
  3. Real-time coach: A coach that is able to advise the social worker on possible ways to handle elderly people based on known historical behaviours or logged cases.
  4. Real-time Duolingo: A language coach that is able to advise social workers on basic language terminologies and meanings based on their conversations.

4.3.2. Trade-off Analysis

In order to decide which concept to prioritise, a trade-off analysis was carried out.

4 key categories were chosen as shown in the table below. The bulk of weightage was allocated to the 4 underserved outcomes. Reduction of time required to do administrative reports was given a lower weightage because it has the lowest opportunity score among all 4 outcomes, as shown in Table 3 above.

For each criterion, a score of 5 was given to the concept which satisfied the criteria most, and 2 for the concept which satisfied the criteria least, as shown in Table 4 above. Fact Assistant had the highest overall score.

4.4. Critical Experience Prototype (CEP) (Rex)

Since Fact Assistant had the highest score, our team created a critical experience prototype to test the Fact Assistant. Although the concept is for Social Workers, we used a befriender to gain quick insights first.

4.4.1. Formation of CEP

Objective: The objective was to see if timely facts about the elderly help with conversational flow for a Social Worker. The test was 20 minutes long.

Parties involved:

  1. The AI: The “AI” provides prompts through text messages, while referring to a fact sheet. He listened to the conversation through earbuds so that he could send factual prompts according to the conversation.
  2. The “Social Worker”: A befriender who role played as a Social Worker. He referred to an iPad for prompts while interacting.
  3. The elderly

An image of the fact sheet used can be found in the appendix.

Post Test:
After the test, questionnaires were handed out to the participants.

4.4.2. Results of CEP

The key findings are shown in Table 5 above. In particular, we found that 45% of prompts were converted to actual topics, and 18% led to enjoyable topics that made the elderly smile. This tells us that prompts can help facilitate conversation with the elderly.

Furthermore, the “Social Worker” was able to achieve a talk ratio of 78% elderly, 22% himself, which is close to the benchmark of 70% patient and 30% talk ratio for therapists. [R3]

We also discovered that some prompts the elderly enjoy discussing can revolve around lotteries, as well as their good old memories. These can be seen in Table 6 above. More information on all the prompts used can be found in the appendix.

4.4.3. Post CEP questionnaire findings

In general, both the “Social Worker” and the elderly found the conversation smooth.

However, the “Social Worker” did mention that it was difficult to use the prompts in certain situations because he could not link the prompt and the current conversation. The questionnaire results can be found in the appendix.

4.4.4. Conclusion of results

In conclusion, the results are promising and they show us that distraction-free factual prompts can aid the “Social Worker” in generating topics for the conversation, and could even potentially lead to enjoyable topics. This directly addresses the respective underserved outcomes.

  1. Increase the number of topics that can be talked about with the elderly so that visits are more conversational.
  2. Increase the confidence of the social worker when dealing with the elderly

4.4.5. Shortcomings of results

However, there are some important limitations to note.

  1. Firstly, the sample size tested is too small. We only conducted it on 1 “Social Worker” and 1 elderly. The small sample size cannot represent the experience of the larger population of elderly and social workers.
  2. Secondly, the tester of our CEP is not the same as the user of our product. The user of our product is meant to be Social Workers in the elderly field.
  3. Lastly, although the elderly and the “Social Worker” are strangers, they know the observer mutually. As a result, the elderly would be less inclined to feel tense because the mutual relationship already helps to establish some form of rapport.

4.5. Future refinement of concepts for Social Workers (Rex)

To address the downside where the user found it hard to draw connections between the current conversation and the fact, we propose combining the Fact Assistant and Real Time Coach concepts to address this. Since the Fact Assistant only provides the user with the what, the Real Time Coach can complement it by providing the how, which helps the user to connect the dots.

4.6. Next steps for social workers (Rex)

Our next steps would be to further validate the findings of the Fact Assistant concept by doing the following:

  1. Firstly, to validate our results, we would need to increase our sample size.
  2. Secondly, we should now test the Fact Assistant concept on actual Social Workers. This would give us a more accurate representation.
  3. Lastly, to reduce the biasness of our tests, it would be important to test them on strangers.

5. Overall Conclusion

In conclusion, our two pronged approach aims to alleviate the elderly loneliness problem through the elderly and the Social Workers.

In general, we discovered that the elderly are receptive towards AI companions. We also discovered that real time prompts have the potential to boost Social Worker interactions with elderly.

Moving forward, more testing and combination of concepts for both approaches are required to further validate our interim findings.

Observation from Befriender at Your Service Critical Function Prototyping

Qualitative Observations (ChatGPT)
Demonstrated basic conversational politeness and willingness to engage once the ice was broken. Broke the ice well with conversations usually surrounding how the day went for the user , how was work , and usually got the attention of the new user straight away. Seemed like ice breaking was not too much of an issue Topical fixation: The AI repeatedly returned to a single topic — work and jobs — up to seven times, causing the conversation to become monotonous and disengaging. Turn-taking and pacing: Frequent pauses, overlaps, and interruptions made the exchange choppy. The AI often spoke over the user, disrupting the participant’s train of thought. Initiative: The model rarely initiated or sustained new conversational threads. The participant had to “wait to start talking,” creating an unnatural rhythm where the user carried the flow. Engagement: Missed emotional cues such as humour and enthusiasm. For example, when the participant cheerfully shared about his marathon experiences, the AI responded with a generic “tell me more,” failing to build on his excitement.
Qualitative Observations (Gemini)
The participant responded better when context was added by relatives(e.g., favourite foods or travel topics). With that context, the AI could maintain a smoother flow and was able to engage the user well. When relatable topics were brought up the user even was seen to be visibly excited to explore more into his/ her interests. Question-driven style: The AI relied heavily on direct questioning, rarely initiating new threads independently. The conversation felt one-way, with repetitive prompts rather than co-created dialogue. Dependence on human context: The model struggled to infer personal preferences without external input. It required Jonathan to guide the conversation by providing relevant context. Bad personalisation: Questions such as “Any plans to relax?” lacked connection to previous responses. When it attempted personalization (e.g., suggesting Bangkok markets or trips), the phrasing sounded scripted and overly formal — “like talking to Wikipedia.”
Qualitative Observations (Claude)
Empathy and sensitivity: Demonstrated empathetic pivots (e.g., “That must have been hard”) and responded compassionately to tough upbringing which got the elderly to nod and acknowledge Contextual memory: Remembered earlier conversation details and referenced them later, helping the participant feel “heard” and understood. This continuity enhanced emotional connection. Emotional fatigue: Although the AI was empathetic, it overused deep reflective prompts. After about ten exchanges, the participant showed subtle frustration and shorter replies, indicating cognitive or emotional fatigue. Topic fixation: Stayed too long in one reflective mode — reminiscence and family stories — without balancing with lighter or forward-looking topics. Limited topic rotation: When the participant shifted to practical, everyday talk, Claude remained empathetic but failed to vary tone or direction.
